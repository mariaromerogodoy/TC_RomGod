Why Trading Cards???
Let’s be so for real: everyone’s got a hobby. Some people collect sneakers. Others binge-watch entire seasons of shows they can’t even remember the plot of. But have you ever sat down, cracked open a fresh deck of cards, and realized… wait a second this is actually kinda fun??!!? Welcome to the secret (but not-so-secret) world of playing cards and trading cards a place where logic, luck, and a little bit of obsession all hang out together.
Young People Need This Hobby
1- You meet people.
Card games are instant icebreakers. You might start as strangers but end the night yelling “DRAW FOUR!” like family.
2- You actually use your brain.
Strategy, memory, risk management it’s like your brain’s version of a gym workout.
3- It’s creative.
Some players make their own decks or invent new rules. You can even design custom cards with your own art.
4- GET OFF YOUR PHONEEE!!!!
No screens, no stress. No notifications. No doomscrolling. Just vibes. Basically, it’s everything your brain needs after too much screen time and not enough imagination.
World Of Cardsss
Long before Pokemon, panini stickers, or pack-cracking YouTube videos, there were playing cards. Their origin isn’t 100% certain, but scholars generally agree that playing cards evolved in China sometime during or after the Tang dynasty (618–907 CE). One often-cited early reference is from the year 868, when a Tang dynasty record mentions a “leaf game” played by a princess. Although that reference is vague, it suggests that leaf-style paper “cards” or tokens were used in games. Over time, variations of these leaf cards may have functioned like “play money” or as game tokens in gambling contexts, eventually evolving into more structured suits and values. From China, cards seem to have spread westward through trade routes, contacts in Persia, India, and the Islamic world, eventually reaching Egypt. By the medieval era, the Mamluk Sultanate in Egypt had decks with suits like polo sticks, coins, swords, and cups. One notable surviving Mamluk-style deck was discovered in the Topkapı Palace, believed to date to the 15th century. Those decks likely influenced the European development of playing cards. The first reliable mention of playing cards in Europe appears around 1371 in Catalonia (Spain), in a rhyme dictionary referencing “naip” (a word for cards). By the late 1300s and early 1400s, multiple European cities issued bans or regulations on card games, suggesting playing cards were spreading and gaining popularity. As cards became more common in Europe, their designs evolved. The Latin suits (cups, coins, swords, batons) survived in southern European decks (Italy, Spain), while German and Swiss regions used suits like leaves, bells, hearts, and acorns. The French eventually simplified the designs into the now-familiar hearts, diamonds, clubs, and spades around the 15th century, which made mass production easier. Mrs Blackwell’s Village Bookshop. Innovations like reversible court cards (so that the king or queen is upright whichever way you hold the card) and corner indexing (printing the value in corners so you can fan the cards) came later (from the 18th to 19th centuries). In the 1600s and onward, guilds and formal “makers of playing cards” were established in cities like London. For example, in 1628, the Mystery of Makers of Playing Cards of the City of London obtained a royal charter. Over the centuries, improvements in paper, printing, coatings, and mass production techniques made playing cards cheaper, more durable, and widely accessible. That’s the story of playing cards in general. But how did we get from those traditional decks to the world of trading cards like Pokémon and companies like Topps and Panini? Let’s follow that thread.
